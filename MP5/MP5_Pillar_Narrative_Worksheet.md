# MP5 Pillar Narrative Worksheet — Team _name_

For each pillar, answer in 3–5 sentences. The answers become the spine of
your presentation — slides illustrate, the narrative carries. Build this
worksheet first, then build slides; resist the temptation to start in
PowerPoint.

This worksheet is **not submitted as a separate artifact**. It exists so
the team thinks the story through together before splitting up the deck.

## Pillar 1: Goal & Direction

**MP1 starting requirements:**
> _What did the original Jordan Chen design brief actually ask for? Pull
> from your MP1 work — envelope, jaw opening range, thumb-wheel turn
> count, materials._

**Final requirements:**
> _What does your unified MP4 design actually meet? Has anything
> tightened, loosened, or moved?_

**What changed and why:**
> _The renegotiation. What got dropped, what got added, what got
> tightened — and what evidence drove the change. "We dropped the
> 0.5 N grip target because the four-bar transmission angles in our
> chosen ratio gave us 0.7 N at the cost of stroke" beats "we adjusted
> requirements as we learned."_

**Strongest example moment:**
> _One specific design decision where Goal & Direction mattered. A meeting
> where the requirement got rewritten, a notebook cell where you walked
> back from a target, a moment where you held the line._

## Pillar 2: Context Management

**Where context saved you:**
> _A moment where MP2 RAG, a skill, or carried-forward knowledge
> prevented a wrong decision. "Our skill caught that we were specifying
> a tolerance the print shop can't hold" beats "the RAG was useful."_

**Where context was missing:**
> _A moment where you had to go find more — a doc that wasn't in the
> corpus, a fact you assumed and had to verify, a question that exposed

**What your team learned about managing context across MPs:**
> _One durable insight. The kind of thing you'd put in a junior
> engineer's onboarding doc._

## Pillar 3: Tools & Integration

**Your team's stack — final form:**
> _Which tools, MCP servers, skills, hosts your team actually used
> together for MP4. Not what you set up and never opened — what you
> reached for repeatedly._

**Live demo plan:**
> _Two pieces, planned together: (1) the mechanism showing motion —
> physical prototype or dynamic CAD/sim — and (2) at least one live MCP
> tool call or function call on a real engineering question relevant to
> your design. Which tool, called on which question, with what expected
> answer. The demo is graded on whether it runs, not on whether the
> answer matches a golden output._

**What you'd build differently if starting over:**
> _One honest answer. Could be a tool you'd skip, a skill you'd write
> earlier, a host you'd pick instead, a piece of plumbing you'd remove._

## Pillar 4: Centaur Engineering

**Strongest centaur loop:**
> _One specific moment where the AI + human combination produced
> something neither would have produced alone. Describe it concretely.
> "On Sunday afternoon I asked the MCP server to derive the
> transmission angle equation; it gave me an answer with the wrong arm
> length. I caught it because it didn't match my hand calc, fed the
> correct geometry back, and got a working implementation in the next
> turn."_

**What each side contributed:**
> _Be honest. What did the AI actually do — derive, suggest, draft, find
> the source? What did the human do — frame, verify, choose, override?
> Why did the combination matter?_

**Where it failed:**
> _Where the loop broke down. A round where you stopped trusting the
> AI's output, a moment where pushing back didn't help, a problem you
> ended up solving alone after trying with the AI._

## Pillar 5: Evaluation & Trust

**The wrong-AI moment** *(the story Jordan asked for explicitly):*
> _The specific instance when the AI was confidently wrong and you
> caught it. Three things to name: what was the claim, what was the
> truth, how did you catch it. Triangulation? Hand calc? Sim
> disagreement? A teammate? Whatever the mechanism, name it._

**Per-subsystem trust call:**
> _Pulled from your MP4 Part B trust assessment. Which subsystems would
> you print tomorrow, which ones wouldn't you, and why. The room cares
> about the asymmetry — confident on the linkage, less so on the gear
> pair, etc._

**What you would verify before mass production:**
> _The prototype-to-production gap, honestly assessed. "Bench test the
> printed gear pair under realistic load — Lewis didn't catch the PLA
> creep mode we'd worry about" beats "more testing is needed."_
